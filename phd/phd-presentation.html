A Stellar Metaphor: Forging the Research Trajectory

The Nebula of Complex Systems Thinking

When I think about complex systems thinking, I am reminded of a dense nebula in space. At the core of this nebula lies complex systems thinking, my research focus that binds everything together. Surrounding this core are diffuse elements, including science education theories, learning technologies, and instructional practices. They form the interstellar medium, providing the context and abundant materials for new insights, much like the conditions necessary for star formation.

Study 1: The Gravitational Force: Systematic review

To construct a star system (my research program), I must first introduce a gravitational force to draw scattered elements together. My first study, a systematic review, serves as this initial gravity. It pulls diverse findings from the literature into a coherent framework, forming a protostar.

Study 2: The Pressure for Fusion: Empirical study

However, a protostar cannot achieve fusion without immense pressure. My second study provides this pressure through empirical validation. During this compression, my initial understanding may be incomplete or imprecise. Recognizing these limitations does not render the work meaningless; rather, it indicates where refinement is needed.

"I must first understand my current reality before I can transcend it to create new value."

Study 3: The Stellar Interaction: Research plan

Once fusion ignites (my research findings achieve coherence), I can look outward to explore how my newly formed star interacts with other celestial bodies. My third study explores how complex systems thinking interacts with students' scientific inquiry.

Research Plan: Purpose

Scientific Inquiry and Complex Systems Thinking

My research purpose is to investigate the interplay between complex systems thinking and students' scientific inquiry throughout the entire inquiry process.

Research Goals

  • Provide empirical evidence about how complex systems thinking shapes students' reasoning and decision-making at different inquiry phases
  • Identify productive patterns in students' engagement with complex systems thinking
  • Recognize persistent challenges in applying complex systems thinking to scientific inquiry
Page 12

Research Plan: Participants

Study Population

This study involved 105 students in the 11th grade (aged 16-17) from a senior high school in Taiwan. The participants were drawn from four randomly selected classes within the same school.

Preparation Phase

To ensure all participants possessed basic complex systems thinking, students first completed a three-week complex systems thinking program.

Main Study Phase

Following this preparation, they participated in an eight-week scientific inquiry unit, which was taught by the same teachers. The instructional sessions took place in a laboratory during their respective class periods.

Research Plan: Data Collection

Data Source

Students from the two inquiry conditions were further divided into twelve groups (six from each condition), with four to five students in each.

During the eight-week inquiry unit, students were asked to self-record their weekly group discussion and transcribed them into transcripts, which served as the primary data for our study.

Research Plan: Epistemic Network Analysis

Analysis Tool: Epistemic Network Analysis (ENA)

I plan to use Epistemic Network Analysis (ENA) to analyze the interplay between scientific inquiry and complex systems thinking.

Why Epistemic Network Analysis?

ENA can model patterns of association among elements in a network over time (Shaffer et al., 2016).

ENA is therefore well-suited to our goal of investigating the interplay between students' scientific inquiry and their complex systems thinking.

Moreover, its affordance for network comparison will allow me to compare the differences between different inquiry conditions.

.

ENA Applications

  • Explore students' conceptual structure (Chang & Tsai, 2023)
  • Examine the co-occurrence of learning behaviors with cognitive processes (Huang et al., 2023)

Analysis Tool: ENA Web Tool (https://www.epistemicnetwork.org/)
