What Is Seen When Using Vision III?

Science and Colonialism

Science has played a prominent role in the colonial process

Historical recognition that scientific development was intertwined with colonial expansion and exploitation.

Environmental Injustices

Environmental injustices: an unequal exposition of already marginalised communities to the impact of environmental degradation

MAPA (Most Affected People and Areas) — those who contributed least to climate change suffer most from its consequences.

Vision III enables us to see the power dynamics, historical injustices, and ongoing inequities embedded within scientific practice and knowledge production.

What the Author Found?

Four Key Take-Away Points in the Paper

1. Science as Intertwined

Understanding science as intertwined with social, political and moral legacies and obligations

Science is not neutral or separate from society—it is deeply embedded in social and political contexts.

2. Who Benefits?

Recognising that complex socio-historical processes impact the production of knowledge in science, who benefits (and who does not) from such knowledge and who is supported to be part of these knowledge communities

Critical examination of power dynamics in knowledge production and distribution.

3. Addressing Insularity

Addressing the insularity of narratives about science, scientific development and voices as emanating only from the Global North

Expanding beyond Western-centric narratives to include diverse knowledge systems and perspectives.

4. Recognising Oppression

Recognising oppressive aspects within historical and contemporary scientific (and technological) developments

Acknowledging how science has been used to justify and perpetuate systems of oppression.

These four insights provide a framework for reimagining NOS education through a critical-decolonial lens, enabling students to engage more deeply with socio-scientific challenges.

Toward Transformative NOS Education

By applying critical-decolonial perspectives to our teaching of Nature of Science, we can empower students to understand not just what science knows, but how and why that knowledge was produced, who benefits from it, and what responsibilities we have in addressing socio-scientific challenges and injustices.
